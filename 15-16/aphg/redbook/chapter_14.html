14D: Transportation and Modern Urban Growth

  • Internal transportation systems most responsible for the modern city
    • Cities rely on accessibility
  • Stages of transportation and cities
    • Walking City
      • First stage
      • Most people walk from place to place
      • Every city before 1850
      • Biggest cities were 2 miles in radius, the longest distance a person could comfortably walk in an hour
      • Growth and inability to expand created density
        • New York
          • 136 people per acre
        • Philadelphia and Boston
          • 83 people per acre
      • Land values also rose
        • Land within 1 mile of center of Chicago was valued at $810,000
    • Streetcar/omnibus/horsecar stage
      • After Civil War cities' population expanded rapidly
      • End of 19th century, electric streetcars made
      • Concentric zone model
        • Ernest Burgess
        • Central business district
        • CBD fringe
          • Industrial belt, warehouse functions
        • Zone of transition
          • Deteriorating housing, cheap hotels
        • Zone of independent workmen's homes
          • Workers who owned their own homes and could have some leisure
        • Residential zone
          • Houses of middle class, low densities, single family houses
        • Commuters zone
          • Suburban towns and satellite cities
      • Sector model
        • Homer Hoyt
        • High rent area
          • Farthest from areas of industry
          • Exclusive shops, white collar services
          • Higher elevations, nice views of city
          • Snob appeal
        • Low rent sectors
          • Not attractive for residential development
          • Where industry and unsightly urban functions were present
          • Slums; disadvantaged ethnic groups
        • Middle-income sectors
          • Between high and low rent sectors
          • Middle class
    • Recreational auto city
      • Third stage
      • Automobiles began as a novelty but became affordable in the middle class
      • 1930 -26 million cars
      • Car expanded radius to 30-40 miles
      • bedroom communities
        • Commuter towns inhabited by people who drive or take public transport to another city for work
    • Fourth stage
      • Final stage
      • After WWII
      • Influx of servicemen
  • Suburbs
    • 1990 half of US lived in suburbs
  • Interstate Highway System
    • 1950
    • Large, limited access superhighways
    • Made it easier to live farther away from cities
  • Auto centered city
    • edge cities
      • New concept for modern cities Provides more jobs than homes
