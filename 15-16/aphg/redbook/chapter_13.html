13C: Urbanization in an Era of Capitalism

  • Urbanization - the increase in the percentage of people who live within cities
    • Cities may expand but may not necessarily be experiencing urbanization
    • Cities originally supported elite class through taxes of the much larger rural population
  • Situation - the way in which a particular place relates to the space that surrounds it
    • Large hinterlands surrounding the cities could import goods from the farms
    • Cities exercised control over the empires
      • Built canals, controlled sea lanes, and developed road networks
  • Capitalist cities emerged in Europe which relied on commerce more than control of political leaders
    • Access to the markets was the central consideration
    • Economic activity no longer required a strong centralized government
    • These new cities helped to form urban networks
      • Cities that are tied together in some meaningful way
      • functional linkages - the meaningful ties between cities in an urban network that were historically political and administrative
        • Based on trade
        • Located on trading routes
      • Hanseatic League
        • Gave shelter to out of town merchants, and various aide to conducting trade

13D: Industrial Cities

  • A new type of economy was required for any large scale urbanization to occur
    • Capitalism could not do this alone
  • Industrialization
    • Labor required to run factories; factories required raw materials and a market
    • Agriculture became more efficient
      • Provide labor in factories and provide food for new urbanites
    • Cities could now access a wider range of raw materials thanks to better transportation
    • Four major effects of industrialization
      • Allowed cities to grow tremendously
      • Enabled proliferation of cities
      • Led to more concentrated settlement patterns
      • Altered logic of urban location
  • Concentration of labor
    • Required by factory systems
    • Resulted from forms of mass production
      • Favored concentrated settlement in some places
    • Factory owners created cheap living quarters for workers
      • 40,000 people per building
      • 8 per room
      • 30 to a toilet

13F: Urbanization Patterns around the World

  • Over half of current population lives in cities
  • Developed countries have about 70% of residents living in cities, while less developed countries have a smaller portion
    • Most less developed countries are experiencing rapid rural to urban movement
  • Urbanization corresponds with economic development
    • Wealthier countries are more likely to undergo transformations that cause rural to urban migration because they may enjoy a more dynamic economy
    • Peculiarities
      • India is large but has low urban percentage
      • Latin America has high urban percentage but lacks economic development
  • Urbanization does not equal population density
    • Bangladesh and the Netherlands are dense but have low urban percentage
    • Canada and Libya have sparse populations but have high urban percentage

13G: Agglomeration Economics and Urban Functions

  • Agglomeration economies
    • Different economic activities tend to locate next to each other and act as a powerful magnet, attracting other activities
    • Includes factories, infrastructure, and services
  • New cities included
    • Suppliers to provide essential materials
    • Transportation facilities
    • Access to financial services
    • Production services
  • Relationship between city and hinterland became complicated as industries could begin to sell only to local residents as they grew
  • Urban functions
    • Specialized functions taken on by cities as a result of interurban networks
    • Wealthiest and most integrated regions
    • American Manufacturing Belt
  • Some functions of cities are more heavily promoted In certain cities
    • LA is center of film and television industry
    • Las Vegas - tourism and conventions
    • Boston - colleges and universities
