Territoriality

  • Most institutions use the subdivision of territory to administer and govern effectively
    • Overall territory is divided into political subunits
  • Territory is used to as a means to manipulate space and make the administration of the institution more efficient
  • 3 positives to subunits
    1. Efficiency
      • Managing a large territory is difficult
      • Locating where problems occur can be made easier with subunits
    2. Flexibility
      • Different units may require varying degrees of administration
      • Dividing them into smaller spatial units provides flexibility
      • Ie. Government establishing different languages within different regions
    3. Responsiveness
      • Administration is able to respond to separate problems within each subunit
      • A governor can respond to a county’s issue better than the US President
  • 2 negatives to subunits
    1. Subunits restrict access to certain people
      • Movement between districts may be strictly controlled
      • Markers are made between territories to act as barriers
    2. Mismatch between scope and effectiveness of solving a problem
      • Results from proliferation of subunits
      • A certain problem may be best handled by a higher level of administration, but the tools required to address the issue are only available to local government or institution
      • Land use should be handled by government, zoning is only available to local jurisdictions
  • Territorial development of the Roman Catholic Church
    • Early church communities did not want to adapt the kind of structure seen in the Jewish religion
    • Establishing territories was difficult because Catholicism was still illegal in Rome
    • When Roman Empire adopted the religion, the territorial protocol changed dramatically
      • The changes remained after the state fell
    • The pope appoints bishops over territories called diocese and archdiocese
      • Global territories were easier to manage but also brought bureaucratic and hierarchical structure

Political Economy and Functions of the Modern State

  • Political economy
    • The relationship among the state, the members of the state, and the economic activities contained within the state
  • The economy is composed of the private and public sector functions
    • Private sector
      • Factories, firms, offices
      • In a capitalist country, they produce goods and services
    • Public sector
      • External relations
      • Judicial system
      • Collects taxes
  • Extraction economy
    • Priorities were in enriching the ruler/ aristocracy and maintaining the army
    • Early kingdoms
    • The people give taxes, the state gives only power
  • Capitalist economy
    • Economy in which a wide variety of private concerns are dominant and the state is far less involved in the production of goods and services
    • Still requires taxes to perform certain functions such as those listed in the public sector
    • People
      • Give taxes to the state
      • Receive government services
      • Give labor and purchases to private sector
      • Receive goods and wages
    • Private enterprises
      • Give taxes to the state
      • Receive regulation and services from the state
      • Receive labor and give wages
      • Receive purchases and give goods
    • State
      • Receives taxes
      • Gives regulation and services
  • Communist economy
    • Developed in 20th century
    • State assumes all economic and social functions
    • Comprehensive control over economic activity
    • People
      • Give labor to private sector
      • Receive wages and services
      • Swayed by the state through force
    • Enterprises
      • Controlled by state
      • Give services and receives labor
    • State
      • Controls people and private sector
  • Mixed economies
    • State that is not exclusively capitalist
    • Government will only control the economic activity that it feels is crucial to the public
    • The state receives taxes, regulates private sector and controls the state enterprises
    • People give and receive labor and goods from both state enterprises and private enterprises
  • Additional roles of the modern state
    • Education
      • Capitalism requires literate and educated citizens
    • Social welfare
      • State originally wanted to punish those in debt, increasingly sought to care for those who couldn’t care for themselves
    • Health care
      • Most industrialized states provide some form of health care
      • US gives care to the elderly and poor
    • Currency
      • Legal tender must be established, printed, and regulated in order for trade to occur
    • Business regulation
      • Private companies must follow certain standards and rules regarding customers, employees, and the environment
    • Infrastructure
      • Communication, transportation, infrastructure required for business
      • Highways and ports

Theories of a State

  • Theories of the state
    • How states operate, particularly within capitalist and democratic systems
  • Pluralist theory of the state
    • Government elected by people and creates/ enforces laws
    • Elections determine prevailing political philosophy
    • Liberal
      • Democratic socialist
      • State provides more services and regulated businesses strictly
    • Neoliberalism
      • Minimal government interference in markets and the promotion of free trade
  • Elite theories of the state
    • State supports elite class of people no matter the original ideology of the constitution
    • Every society has elite who support that state and are favored
    • Not necessarily the same as Marxism
  • Marxist theories of the state
    • The state promotes capitalism and those who control means of production
    • It embeds itself within the modes of production
    • Supports key figures who represent capitalism and its interests
  • Authoritarian states
    • AKA Autocratic states
    • All political power is within a single individual or clique
      • Group known as oligarchy
    • Most premodern states
    • Totalitarianism
      • Associated with fascism, communism, and religious fundamentalism
      • State serves structures in power
      • No opposition to the party allowed
      • Sometimes try to create illusion of democracy
    • Difference between totalitarianism and authoritarian states lies within the degrees of control in the state
    • All autocratic states give people little to no say in governance

Citizenship

  • Subjects
    • Residents of a state in monarchical, feudal, or imperial settings
    • Few rights
  • Citizenship
    • Came into use during Middle Ages in European trading cities
    • Embodies the rights and responsibilities the residents of a state have
    • Political rights through voting
    • Civil rights in free speech etc.
    • Some have economic and social rights
  • Drawbacks
    • Most residents in medieval societies were not considered for citizenship
    • Immigrants may reside but still require full citizenship
    • African Americans had few political or civil rights before 1960
  • Jus Sanguinis
    • Citizenship based off the status of your parents
    • Discriminates against children of foreigners
    • Germany
      • Person can be born in another country and still be a citizen as long as parents are
      • Child of foreigner born in Germany cannot be a German citizen
  • Jus Soli
    • Citizenship gained through being born in the country
    • United States
      • Children of immigrants, including illegals, are granted citizenship if born within the territory
      • Can be controversial
  • Dual citizenship
    • Person is a citizen of two countries
    • Can occur because of different countries’ protocol for citizenship
    • German baby born in United States could be both German and American

Subdividing the State

  • Political subunits are divided into a hierarchy
  • First order subunit
    • State, province, prefecture, district, etc.
  • Subsequent develop of political subunits
    • Over time, subunits form because of preexisting domains or sociocultural divisions
  • Political units established before the dominant population has been settled may eventually push for greater rights because of their unique personalities
  • 2 relationships between federal gov. and first order subunits
    • Unitary states
      • Power belongs to the central government
      • Subunits have little control over
        • How they are governed
        • Who they are individually governed by
        • Their boundaries
        • Their constitutional rights
      • Are democratic
      • Devolution
        • Process in which the central gov. gives power to lower subunits
        • Seen in the Soviet Union
    • Federal states
      • Subunits have independent constitutional authority
      • Central state conducts foreign affairs and has overall higher level of power
      • Subunits are able to
        • Pass laws
        • Have independent government and legislation
      • Soviet Union/ Russia
        • Creates stability between rival ethnic groups by giving each national group some form of territory
      • 7/8 largest countries are federal
        • Argentina, Australia, Brazil, Canada, India, Russia, USA
  • Confederations
    • Each state agrees to give up some powers in order to work together, while still retaining large amounts of power
    • Unites States began this way but gave up as to lack of a central power
    • Most confederation have fallen or failed
      • Switzerland and Canada became more federal
      • United Arab Republic attempted but failed
  • Asymmetric federalism
    • Canadian
    • Quebec has more power than other provinces
      • Language laws
      • Pension system
      • Immigration control

Cores and capitals

  • Core region
    • Where the state and dominant nationality emerged
    • Accretion
      • Process of the state expanding from the core
    • Some states have multiple cores
    • Contains population, economic, and cultural focus
  • Capital city / capital region
    • May be the core
      • Depends on how logical the location is
    • Some states have multiple capitals that fulfil different roles
    • Location can be symbolic and controversial
  • Introduced capitals
    • States create new city as capital or designate smaller city instead of using a preexisting one
    • Reflects an orientation toward future instead of past
    • Compromise capital
      • Chosen by agreement from each subunit
      • Ottawa, Canada
      • Washington, D.C.
  • Forward capitals
    • Capitals meant to move the population to less populated areas
    • Brasilia, Brazil

Peripheral and Special Regions

  • Peripheral areas
    • Areas at the edge of political control
    • Opposite of core areas
    • Exclave
      • Part of a country’s territory physically separated by another country
      • Russian area Kaliningrad surrounded by Lithuania, Poland, and Baltic Sea
    • Enclave
      • Country or part of a country completely surrounded by another country
      • Berlin (West Germany) surrounded by East Germany during Cold War
    • Special regions
      • Name for peripheral regions given different powers than the other subunits
      • Ie. Capital Regions
      • Receives special treatment because the central government must work harder to control/ accommodate it
        • Frontier regions
    • United States
      • Has several special regions
      • Territories
        • Areas with power but under control of the US
      • Puerto Rico
        • No taxation/ no representation
        • Controls own internal affairs
    • UK
      • Isle of man
      • Channel Islands
    • Italy
      • 5 autonomous regions
      • North
        • Trentino Alto-Adige, Friuli-Venezia Giulia, Val d’Aosta
        • Culturally distinct
      • South
        • Sicily and Sardinia
        • Share historical circumstances
  • Reservation
    • Established territory for indigenous people
    • Small fraction of previously inhabited land that settlers probably did not want
    • Level of autonomy varies
      • Can make separate constitutions but must follow federal law
      • Have exploited this by crating casinos

Electoral Geography

  • Democratic societies have a system in which citizens' political preferences can be turned into electoral action
  • Pure democracy
    • All citizens have a say in all the issues pertaining to their community
  • Representative democracy
    • People elect representatives who know the issues and represent their interests
  • Electoral geography
    • How political preferences are manifested in representation
    • Political maps
      • Shows the results of an election based on area
      • Electoral geographers interested in how outcome was affected by geography or why certain areas benefit a specific political intereest over another
  • Interpreting election outcomes
    • Strong and weak points of a certain party
    • Greatest degrees of change within a district
    • Districts that represent nationwide preferences
    • How do spatial factors relate to
      • Religion
      • Economics
      • Language
      • Ethnicity
  • Neighborhood effect
    • Contextual effect
    • People's electoral behavior is directly influenced by the surrounding community
    • Certain characteristics of people determine political preference
    • Some studies show that where a person lives is just as important as who they are
      • Community is a place of interaction, affiliation, and particularity

Electoral systems and manipulation

  • Single member plurality system
  • Majoritarian system
  • List system
  • Mixed system
  • Malapportionment
    • Some electoral districts vary in size but remain equal in representation
  • Gerrymandering
    • Manipulation in which support for a particular party is concentrated in one area and diluted in many other areas
